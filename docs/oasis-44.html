The Oasis 44: A Grand Opening for Cleveland's Underground Royalty

Inside the Slavic Village studio where holistic tea, live poetry, and a private album listening session opened a new chapter for Cleveland’s creative health movement.

The door at 6005 Francis Avenue doesn’t announce itself. There is no neon marquee, no velvet rope, no bouncer checking names against a clipboard. There is only a door — slightly ajar on a February evening in Cleveland’s Slavic Village — and behind it, the low hum of something sacred about to begin. This is The Oasis 44. And on the night of February 22nd, 2026, it opened its doors for the first time.

The space belongs to Rosh — founder, curator, and quiet architect of what may become one of the most important wellness and literacy hubs on Cleveland’s southeast side. The Oasis 44 is not a spa. It is not a gallery. It is not a performance venue. It is, somehow, all of these things at once — a health and wellness studio built on a radical premise: that literacy, art, and holistic healing are not separate disciplines but three branches of the same tree.

Its core mission is direct and unflinching: literacy teaching for Cleveland’s minority men. In a city where the adult literacy rate hovers below the national average, where entire zip codes have been written off by institutions that were supposed to serve them, The Oasis 44 is an act of defiance disguised as an invitation.

The Gathering

The grand opening was hosted by Irie Veesh, whose presence set the tone for the evening — warm, intentional, unhurried. Accompanied by Anthony Jackson, Veesh moved through the room like a conductor before the downbeat, making introductions, adjusting the energy, ensuring that every person who crossed the threshold understood they had entered a space that was designed for them.

The air smelled of holistic wellness teas — blends that Rosh had curated for the occasion, served in ceramic cups that felt deliberate in your hands. There were no cocktails. No open bar. This was a different kind of gathering. The kind where the drink is meant to heal you, not help you forget.

Poets stepped to the microphone with the quiet confidence of people who have been writing in private and waiting for a room worthy of their words. Live musicians filled the spaces between sets — not background music, but foreground music, the kind that makes you stop mid-conversation and listen. Artist showcases lined the walls, turning the studio into a gallery of Cleveland’s underground talent — paintings, mixed media, and visual work that spoke the same language as the poetry: honest, unpolished, alive.

The Private Listening Session

Then the room went quiet. ErcoBeatz, who had been working the boards all evening, stepped forward to introduce the headliner: a private listening session of Bryson Lee’s debut album, Time Will Tell.

The lights dimmed. The first track filled the room from the speakers like smoke — slow, deliberate, layered. Nobody reached for their phone. Nobody whispered. The album played through in its entirety, and the room held it the way you hold something fragile that someone has trusted you with. Time Will Tell is not a record that asks for your attention. It commands it — and on this night, in this room, surrounded by the people who built the underground that made it possible, the album found its congregation.

When the final track faded, Kazury took the stage for a live performance of “More Life” — a song that, in the context of the evening, felt less like a closer and more like a benediction. The crowd didn’t cheer immediately. They exhaled first. Then the applause came — not loud, but deep.

Why These Spaces Matter

Cleveland has never lacked talent. What it has lacked — chronically, systematically — are the rooms to put it in. The stages. The studios. The gathering places where the creative class can convene behind closed doors and reveal a window into their world through an artistic medium.

The Oasis 44 is that room.

It is sacred when a community gathers this way. Not for a product launch. Not for a brand activation. Not for content. But for the simple, ancient act of sharing what you’ve made with the people who understand why you made it. The generations that came before — the poets who never had a microphone, the painters who never had a wall, the musicians who never had a listening session — they are the reason these spaces matter. The Oasis 44 gives them voice, retroactively and going forward.

Rosh has built something that Cleveland’s Slavic Village has needed for a long time: a place where wellness is not separated from culture, where literacy is not separated from art, and where the door is open to anyone willing to walk through it.

The oasis, it turns out, was never a metaphor. It was an address.

6005 Francis Avenue. Cleveland, Ohio. 44127.
